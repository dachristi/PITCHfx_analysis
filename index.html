Analytics: Strike Zone Grid Analysis

Since batters and pitchers rarely (if ever) play against other, we cannot directly analyze historical matchups to make predictions. Instead, we develop a profile for the batter and pitcher based on all of the pitches they have ever seen or thrown, and employ statistical analysis to develop the matchup predictions.

The profiles for batters and pitchers are based on the strike zone. From the historical data that we do have, we can see where each pitcher tends to throw the ball and where each batter tends to hit the ball.

The heat maps below show the profiles developed for batters and pitchers. For batters, there are 4 heat maps depicting the tendency to hit a ball in-play, have a called strike, hit a foul ball, and have a called ball. Imagine if you are the pitcher, you would want to know where each opposing batter is most likely to have a called strike so you can throw the ball in that area.





Similarly for pitchers, we can break down the strike zone into 13 cells and look at the relative frequency of where each pitcher tends to throw the ball. Once we profiled the batter and pitcher this way, we simulate 10,000 at-bats while considering ball-strike counts. The results are displayed in the Matchup Engine above.




















Data Acquisition Get the Dataset

The data used for this analysis was obtained by scraping MLB's PITCHf/x XML datasets. The PITCHf/x dataset is created with 2 high speed cameras that capture over 2-dozen features of each pitch thrown in a Major League Baseball game since 2007. The features include start and end speeds, spin rate of the ball, breaking angle, zone the ball crosses above home plate, and more. An in-depth article on the topic can be found at http://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/pitchtracker.html

The data is publically available on the MLB Gameday site. Data was scraped from the MLB site with a Python script that I created and is available on Github. In the repository you will also find a handfull of scripts I wrote to wrangle the data and create graphics.

The final piece was to find current players. A second web-scraping script was developed to gather information from the Wikipedia page showing the 2016 roster.
